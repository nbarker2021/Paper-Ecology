## Definitions

**Definition 1 (Reasoned reapplication).** The *reasoned reapplication* of standard formalism is the cross-domain closure table: the systematic application of standard math, physics, CS, numerical analysis, and formal-methods knowledge to the open math obligations before any claim is called residual.

**Definition 2 (Cross-domain closure table).** The *cross-domain closure table* is the lattice closure (Paper 9) across the domains: the table must close under the intersection of all domains, meaning that every claim is verified by at least one domain. The terminal address is the verified claim.

**Definition 3 (Non-explained remainder).** The *non-explained remainder* is the open obligation: the part of the open math that is not explained by the standard formalism. It is the boundary (Paper 5) between the standard formalism and the open math.

**Definition 4 (Theory admission gate).** The *theory admission gate* (Paper 12) is the gate that admits only those claims that are verified by standard knowledge; the remainder is the open obligation. The reasoned reapplication is the theory admission gate applied to all standard formalism.

**Definition 5 (Lattice code chain as domain hierarchy).** The *lattice code chain* 1→3→7→8→24→72 (Paper 4) underlies the domain hierarchy:
- 1 = mathematics (the foundation);
- 3 = physics (the 3 spatial dimensions);
- 7 = computer science (the 7 layers of the OSI model);
- 8 = numerical analysis (the 8 standard numerical methods);
- 24 = formal methods (the 24 logical rules);
- 72 = open math (the 72 open problems).

**Definition 6 (Domain weights).** The *domain weights* are the VOA weight assignments (Paper 16) for each domain: mathematics w=1, physics w=3, computer science w=7, numerical analysis w=8, formal methods w=24, open math w=72. The weights are the "mass" of each domain in the closure table.

**Definition 7 (Knowledge reuse).** *Knowledge reuse* is the reapplication of standard formalism: the knowledge stored in one domain is reapplied to another domain when the domains share a common structure. The CAM crystal projectors (Paper 28) store the knowledge; the corpus ribbon (Paper 29) transfers it.

**Definition 8 (Analogical reasoning as cross-domain mapping).** *Analogical reasoning* is the cross-domain mapping: the analogy between two domains is the map that preserves the structure, and the reapplication is the transfer of knowledge along this map.

**Definition 9 (Receipt structure enabling reapplication).** The *FLCR receipt structure* (Paper 11) enables reasoned reapplication: the receipts record the knowledge in a domain-independent form, and the reapplication is the replay of the receipts in a new domain.
