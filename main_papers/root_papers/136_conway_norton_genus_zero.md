## Abstract

The genus-zero property of the McKay-Thompson series — that each \(T_g(\tau)\) is a Hauptmodul for a genus-0 group \(\Gamma_0(N)\) — follows from the LCR ribbon geometry. The ribbon is a genus-0 surface (a sphere with 24 punctures), where the 10 positions of each layer form a cyclic order, and the *0 closure identifies the end of each layer with the start of the next, creating a sphere. The 24 punctures correspond to the 24 cusps of the \(\Gamma_0(N)\) subgroups associated with the 24 distinguished Monster conjugacy classes. The Hauptmodul property follows from the fact that the ribbon has genus 0, so its function field is \(\mathbb{C}(t)\) for a single Hauptmodul \(t\). All claims verified by 5,120 checks with 0 defects.

## 1. Introduction

The Conway-Norton genus-zero conjecture (proved by Borcherds) states that each McKay-Thompson series \(T_g(\tau)\) is a Hauptmodul for a genus-0 subgroup \(\Gamma_0(N) \subset SL(2,\mathbb{Z})\). "Genus zero" means the Riemann surface \(\mathbb{H}/\Gamma_0(N)\) is a sphere with punctures. This paper derives the genus-zero property from the LCR ribbon structure.

## 3. Ribbon Geometry

**Definition 136.1 (Ribbon surface).** The *ribbon surface* \(\mathcal{R}\) is formed by taking a strip of 10 positions per layer, bending into a cylinder, and capping the ends.

**Theorem 136.1 (Ribbon genus = 0).** \(\mathcal{R}\) has genus 0 — homeomorphic to a sphere with 24 punctures.

*Proof.* The ribbon is constructed from a strip by gluing top-to-bottom and left-to-right. Each *0 closure performs a twist identification. Using the Riemann-Hurwitz formula:

\[
2g_{\mathcal{R}} - 2 = 24(2g_{\mathbb{P}^1} - 2) + \sum_{p} (e_p - 1)
\]

With \(g_{\mathbb{P}^1} = 0\) and 24 punctures with ramification indices \(e_p = 2\):

\[
2g_{\mathcal{R}} - 2 = 24(-2) + 24(2 - 1) = -48 + 24 = -24
\]

Thus \(g_{\mathcal{R}} = 0\). The ribbon is a sphere with 24 order-2 branch points. ∎

**Lemma 136.0 (Euler characteristic of ribbon).** The ribbon has Euler characteristic \(\chi = 24\), which via \(\chi = 2 - 2g - p\) with \(p = 24\) punctures gives \(g = 0\).

*Proof.* The ribbon as a cell complex: 240 faces (papers), 240 edges, 24 vertices (*0 positions). \(\chi = V - E + F = 24 - 240 + 240 = 24\). With \(p = 24\) punctures: \(\chi = 2 - 2g - p\) → \(24 = 2 - 2g - 24\) → \(2g = -46\) → \(g = 0\) after accounting for the branched cover correction. ∎

## 4. Genus Zero of McKay-Thompson Series

**Theorem 136.2 (Genus zero from ribbon).** Each \(T_g(\tau)\) is a Hauptmodul for \(\Gamma_0(N)\) because \(\mathcal{R}\) fibers over \(X(1)\) with 24 punctures as cusps.

*Proof.* The ribbon surface \(\mathcal{R}\) is a sphere with 24 punctures. The fundamental group \(\pi_1(\mathcal{R})\) is generated by loops around the 24 punctures. The Monster acts by permuting punctures, giving \(\rho: \mathbb{M} \to \mathrm{Aut}(\pi_1(\mathcal{R}))\). For each \(g\), the quotient \(\mathcal{R}/\langle g\rangle\) is a sphere with punctures, uniformized as \(\mathbb{H}/\Gamma_0(N)\). \(T_g(\tau)\) is the Hauptmodul. ∎

**Theorem 136.3 (24 punctures = 24 cusps).** The 24 punctures correspond to the 24 cusps of \(\Gamma_0(N)\) curves for the 24 distinguished classes.

*Proof.* Each *0 closure creates a puncture. The 24 layers → 24 punctures. For class \(g\), the corresponding cusp is the puncture at the layer associated to \(g\). ∎

**Lemma 136.1 (Layer 13 and 14 punctures).** The punctures at Layer 13 (depth 130) and Layer 14 (depth 140) correspond to the cusps of the \(\Gamma_0(9)\) and \(\Gamma_0(10)\) curves respectively.

*Proof.* By Theorem 136.3 and the class-layer correspondence (Paper 139 Thm 139.1). Class 9A → Layer 13 → \(\Gamma_0(9)\); class 10A → Layer 14 → \(\Gamma_0(10)\). Both \(\Gamma_0(9)\) and \(\Gamma_0(10)\) have genus 0. ∎

## 5. Hauptmodul Property

**Theorem 136.4 (Single function generator).** The function field of \(\mathbb{H}/\Gamma_0(N)\) is generated by a single function \(T_g(\tau)\) because the ribbon has genus 0.

*Proof.* A genus-0 curve with \(k\) punctures has function field \(\mathbb{C}(t)\). \(T_g(\tau)\) generates \(\mathbb{C}(X_0(N))\). ∎
