We suggest that the chiral (e8)1 theory--in many senses the simplest VOA--may have Haagerup symmetry Hi for i = 1, 2, 3. Likewise, we suggest that the non-chiral (E8)1 WZW model may have Hi × Hiop symmetry, and that gauging the diagonal symmetry gives a c = 8 theory with Z(H3) symmetry, which is the theory predicted in [1]. Along the way, we show that (E8)1 also has a Fib × Fibop symmetry, and that gauging the diagonal symmetry gives the (G2)1 × (F4)1 WZW model, explaining the well-known conformal embedding (G2)1 × (F4)1  (E8)1. Finally, we suggest a relation to theories with H3 symmetry at c = 2, 6, complimenting the discussion with new modular bootstrap results.

Symmetry has long been a central organizing principle in theoretical physics, underlying phenomena from crystalline order to particle interactions. Traditional notions of symmetry are rooted in groups of transformations that act on physical systems, but recent advances in quantum field theory and condensed matter physics have revealed a richer landscape of "non-invertible" symmetries, which cannot be described by ordinary groups, instead generalizing to algebraic structures known as fusion categories. Among these, the Haagerup categories [2, 3] stand out for being the simplest examples resisting realization in familiar quantum theories. In this work, we propose that the conformal field theory associated with the exceptional Lie algebra E8 at level one--one of the simplest two-dimensional theories--naturally hosts Haagerup-type symmetry. These results provide a concrete new setting in which exotic symmetry structures may emerge in otherwise well-known quantum systems.

In significantly older work, Evans and Gannon argued for the existence of c  8Z theories with Z(H3) symmetry, with Z(H3) being the Drinfeld center of H3 [1]. Though they were unable to give a complete construction of such theories, they were able to use the modular data of Z(H3) to constrain the modular characters, and in the case of c = 8 they noticed an intriguing connection to the chiral (e8)1 theory, as well as its sub-VOA (e6)1 × (a2)1.
In the current work, we offer an explanation for this connection at the level of the non-chiral (E8)1 WZW model; to avoid confusion, we will denote the chiral theory by (e8)1, and the non-chiral theory by (E8)1. In particular, we will suggest that (E8)1 has an H3 × H3op symmetry 2, and that gauging the diagonal H3 symmetry (using recent techniques for non-invertible gauging developed in e.g. [12­15]) gives the putative Z(H3) theory predicted by Evans and Gannon. The role of the invertible Z3 element in the diagonal H3 is played by the Z3B conjugacy class in E8, whose centralizer is E6 × A2.
We will also see that (e8)1 is self-dual under certain gaugings, which would imply that (e8)1 has Hi symmetry for i = 1, 2, 3, as well as a number of additional duality defects. Finally, we will discuss connections to the c  2 theory predicted in [8­11], suggest the existence of a c  6 counterpart, and compliment the discussion with new modular bootstrap results, summarized in Figures 2 and 3. Details will be given in upcoming work [16].

I. WARM-UP: AUTOMORPHISMS OF E8

By far the most well-understood class of symmetries in two-dimensional rational conformal field theories (RCFTs) are those generated by Verlinde lines [17, 18]. Verlinde lines commute not only with the Virasoro generators, but also with the full chiral algebra of the RCFT. It is the latter property which enables their straightforward classification, and for diagonal RCFTs they can be shown to be in one-to-one correspondence with RCFT primaries, further sharing the same fusion rules.
However, Verlinde lines are not the only symmetries of RCFTs. Indeed, there can be a variety of line operators which are topological, but which do not commute with the chiral algebra. The current letter will focus on a certain class of such symmetries in the (E8)1 WZW model.3
